Core Concepts:
The Five Pillars

These five concepts underpin every signal, every condition, and every trade decision in the dashboard. Understanding them deeply allows you to interpret signals correctly — and to trust the system when it tells you to sit out. Formulas, mechanics, intuitions, and trader-facing implications are all here.

C1

Gamma Exposure (GEX) Panel BGate C#1

GEX is the single most important structural signal in the system and the concept that the dashboard was fundamentally designed around. It quantifies the aggregate gamma position of all market makers (dealers) across the index options market — and therefore predicts the direction and magnitude of their hedging activity.

What Gamma Is

Gamma (Γ) is the second derivative of an option's price with respect to the underlying spot price. It measures how fast an option's delta changes as spot moves. When a dealer writes (sells) an option, they inherit the gamma risk of that option. They must continuously delta-hedge by buying or selling the underlying to stay delta-neutral.

Dealer Hedging Rule (Long Gamma = Sells into up-moves)
If spot ↑ → delta of option rises → dealer who sold option must sell underlying to stay neutral
If spot ↓ → delta falls → dealer must buy underlying

Result: Dealers with large long gamma positions naturally create mean-reversion pressure — they sell into rallies and buy into dips, suppressing directional moves.

The GEX Formula

Per-row GEX contribution (computed in processor.py)
gex_k = Gamma × OI × Spot² × 0.01 × direction_factor

Direction factor
direction_factor = +1 for Puts (dealer long gamma via short puts)
direction_factor = −1 for Calls (dealer long gamma via short calls… simplified)

Net GEX at snapshot (aggregated in analytics.py)
Net_GEX = SUM(gex_k) across all strikes in ATM window

Spot² × 0.01 is the scaling factor to convert from per-point gamma to ₹ notional terms per 1% spot move.

GEX and the Option Buyer: The Key Insight

High Positive GEX = BAD for buyers

A large positive Net GEX means dealers hold massive long gamma. They aggressively sell every up-move and buy every down-move. The market oscillates. Options expire worthless. Directional buying is a losing game in this regime. The premium you buy into a "trending" move gets wiped by the reversal. DO NOT buy options when Net GEX is at or near its daily peak.

Low / Declining GEX = GOOD for buyers

As GEX declines, dealer hedging pressure diminishes. Price can trend without the mean-reversion headwind from dealer flow. The sweet spot for option buyers is when GEX has fallen more than 30% from the day's peak — the pin is breaking. This is when Gate condition #1 fires and the entry window opens.

GEX Regimes Summary

Regime GEX Level Dealer Behaviour Price Behaviour Buyer Strategy
LONG GAMMA High positive (peak) Counter-trend hedging (sell rallies, buy dips) Narrow range, mean-reverting Sit out. Premiums decay. No directional move sustains.
TRANSITIONAL 30–70% of peak (declining) Reduced hedging — dealers liquidating or expiry approaching Range expanding. Trending moves beginning. Monitor closely. Begin pre-entry analysis. Gate condition fires here.
NEUTRAL / LOW <50% of peak Minimal hedging. Near-zero gamma effect. Free-flowing. Trends sustain. Volatility spikes possible. Ideal entry window. Maximum confidence in directional option buying.
SHORT GAMMA Negative Net GEX Pro-trend hedging (buy rallies, sell dips) — amplifies moves Explosive moves. Gaps. Sudden reversals. Very fast moves possible. Extremely tight stops required. High reward, high risk.
ℹ️GEX does not predict direction. A high GEX day is neutral on direction — the market can pin at any level. GEX only tells you the force suppressing or enabling moves. For direction, always combine GEX regime with V_CoC (see Concept C2) and PCR (C5).
C2

Cost of Carry & Velocity V_CoC Panel CGate C#2

Cost of Carry (CoC) measures how far futures are trading above or below the spot index. In an arbitrage-free market, futures trade at a fair premium determined by the risk-free rate minus dividend yield, scaled by time to expiry. When large institutional participants aggressively buy or sell futures beyond this fair value, the carry changes — and the rate of that change is the V_CoC signal.

Why Futures Carry Reveals Institutional Flow

Retail investors primarily trade options. Institutional investors (FIIs, large proprietary desks) primarily build directional views via futures, because futures have higher liquidity, no premium cost, and unlimited theoretical payoff. This means the futures carry is the cleanest signal of institutional intent available without access to prime broker data.

Fair Carry Calculation (theoretical)
Fair_CoC = Spot × (r − d) × (DTE / 365)
r = risk-free rate (~6.5% for India), d = dividend yield (~1.2% for NIFTY)
For NIFTY with 22,000 spot and 20 DTE: Fair_CoC ≈ 22,000 × 0.053 × (20/365) ≈ ₹63

Observed CoC
CoC_t = Futures_LTP_t − Spot_t

15-Minute Velocity
V_CoC_t = CoC_t − CoC_(t−3) /* 3 snapshots × 5 min = 15 minutes */

The Institutional Sequence

When an FII desk decides to build a large short position in NIFTY, the sequence of observable events is:

  1. T+0: Desk begins selling NIFTY futures in tranches (to minimise market impact). Futures premium starts shrinking → CoC drops → V_CoC turns negative.
  2. T+15 to T+30 min: V_CoC crosses −10. Gate condition #2 fires. Alert Feed shows VCOC_BEARISH event.
  3. T+45 to T+90 min: Futures OBI turns negative (order book fills with more sellers). Gate condition #3 fires. Gate score rises to ≥4.
  4. T+90 to T+120 min: Spot index finally responds — begins declining as futures discount forces arbitrageurs to sell spot. The move option buyers capture.

Why the 60–90 Minute Lead Time Exists

Institutional desks execute large orders in tranches over 30–90 minutes to avoid moving the market against themselves. The futures carry change is visible in each 5-minute snapshot as positions accumulate. By the time the full position is built and the spot market responds, a skilled observer (you, reading V_CoC) already knew the direction well in advance. This lead time is the structural edge this dashboard exploits.

Bullish vs. Bearish V_CoC Signals

V_CoC Level Direction Implied Gate Impact Recommended Position Type
< −15 Strongly bearish — aggressive FII selling Gate C#2 fires PUT options (PE) on the underlying
−10 to −15 Bearish — institutional sellers dominant Gate C#2 fires PE — confirm with GEX <70% of peak
−10 to +10 Neutral — no institutional conviction No Gate impact Sit out or very small speculative entry
+10 to +15 Bullish — institutional buyers dominant Bullish Gate fires CALL options (CE)
> +15 Strongly bullish — aggressive FII buying Bullish Gate fires CE — maximum confidence

Implied Volatility (IV) Panel GGate C#5

Implied Volatility is the market's consensus forecast of future price movement expressed as an annualised standard deviation percentage. It is "implied" because it is reverse-engineered from the option market price using the Black-Scholes model — you observe the price, then solve backwards to find the volatility assumption that justifies it.

What IV Means in Practical Terms

Expected Move Formula (1 Standard Deviation, 1 Day)
Expected_Daily_Move = Spot × (IV / 100) / √252

Example: NIFTY at 22,500 with IV = 15%:
Daily move = 22,500 × 0.15 / √252 = 22,500 × 0.15 / 15.87 ≈ ±₹213
The market is pricing in ~±213 points as a 1σ daily range.

As an option buyer, IV has a direct and non-linear effect on your P&L:

  • Low IV (IVP <50%): You buy at below-median premium. Any subsequent IV expansion (rise in IV) adds profit on top of your delta gain. Your option becomes worth more even if spot doesn't move as far as expected.
  • High IV (IVP >70%): You buy at above-median premium. You need a very large spot move to overcome the theta decay and the risk of IV compression back toward the mean. Even a correct directional call can result in a loss if IV "crushes" post-event.

Volatility Skew (Why OTM Puts Are More Expensive)

In a theoretical Black-Scholes world, IV would be the same for all strikes. In reality, OTM puts consistently trade at higher IV than equivalent OTM calls. This "put skew" exists because institutional investors continuously purchase OTM puts as tail-risk hedges, creating persistent excess demand that keeps put premiums elevated.

Strike Zone Typical IV vs ATM Implication for Buyer
Deep OTM Puts (−4 to −8 strikes) 20–40% higher IV than ATM Very expensive. Rarely worth buying — theta erosion and IV skew premium destroy returns unless the move is very large very fast.
Slight OTM Puts (−1 to −3 strikes) 5–15% higher IV than ATM Acceptable. The skew premium is manageable if IVP overall is <50%.
ATM (0 strikes) Baseline Preferred strike region for buying. Maximum delta efficiency, minimal skew penalty.
OTM Calls (+1 to +3 strikes) 0–5% lower IV than ATM Slightly cheaper than puts on the IV surface. Call buying is marginally more IV-efficient than put buying in normal markets.
⚠️IV crush risk: After scheduled events (RBI policy, election results, quarterly results), IV often drops 30–60% within minutes of the announcement. If you hold long options through the event, the delta gain from the spot move must exceed the vega loss from IV crush. Always check the IV Surface panel for inflated near-term IV before any known event date.

IVR & IVP — IV Rank & Percentile Gate C#5Screener

IVR and IVP both answer the question "Is today's IV high or low relative to history?" They are different ways of measuring the same thing and have importantly different interpretations.

IV Rank (IVR) — Position in Recent Range
IVR = (Current_IV − 90day_Low_IV) / (90day_High_IV − 90day_Low_IV) × 100

IV Percentile (IVP) — Fraction of Days Below Current IV
IVP = Count(days in last 90 where IV < Current_IV) / 90 × 100

IVR vs IVP: The Critical Difference

IVR — Range Position

Distorted by outliers. If IV spiked to 50% once during a crisis and is now at 18% in a 14–50% range, IVR = (18−14)/(50−14) = 11% — suggesting IV is "cheap". But that 50% spike was a rare tail event that inflates the range. IVR is misleading in these cases.

IVP — True Percentile ✓

Resistant to outliers. If IV was below 18% on only 15 of the last 90 days, IVP = 17% — correctly identifying that IV is cheap. IVP is the preferred metric in this dashboard. Gate condition #5 uses IVP, not IVR.

IVP Interpretation Table

IVP Range Interpretation Option Buyer Action
0–25% Historically very cheap IV. Options in bottom quartile of 90-day distribution. Best time to buy. Maximum IV expansion potential. Long premium strategies highly favoured.
25–50% Below-median IV. Options are cheaper than average. Good time to buy. Gate condition #5 fires. IV is not working against you.
50–70% Above-median IV. Options are slightly expensive. Proceed with caution. Increase size only for very high Gate scores (7+). Prefer shorter expiries to reduce vega exposure.
70–85% High IV historically. Options are expensive. IV mean-reversion likely. Avoid buying. Any purchase is fighting IV headwinds. Event-driven plays only with immediate catalyst identified.
85–100% Extreme IV. Near the highest level in the last 90 trading days. Do not buy options under any circumstance. IV crush will overwhelm any directional gain.
ℹ️Data requirement for IVP: IVP calculation requires at least 10 trading days of IV history to be meaningful (minimum 10 data points in the percentile calculation). With only the 4-day backfill on day one, IVP values are unreliable. They become more accurate after 2–3 weeks of live data accumulation. The dashboard colour-codes IVP in amber when fewer than 10 days of history exist.

Put-Call Ratio & PCR Divergence Panel EGate C#4

The Put-Call Ratio (PCR) is a sentiment indicator measuring the relative demand for puts vs calls. The dashboard tracks two PCRs simultaneously to separate retail short-term sentiment from structural institutional positioning — and exploits the gap between them.

PCR by Volume (Day's Trading Activity)
PCR_vol = Total Put Volume / Total Call Volume (today, across ATM window strikes)

PCR by OI (Structural/Institutional Positioning)
PCR_oi = Total Put OI / Total Call OI (cumulative, across ATM window strikes)

PCR Divergence (Gate Condition #4)
PCR_div = |PCR_vol − PCR_oi| → fires when > 0.25

The Retail Trap Mechanism

The most actionable PCR signal is high divergence with Vol-PCR > OI-PCR: retail traders are buying puts aggressively today (high volume PCR), but the structural OI-PCR is not confirming excess put positioning. This pattern frequently precedes a sharp upward reversal — the retail crowd buys puts at the wrong time, and the unwinding of those losing puts creates buying pressure that accelerates the up-move (the "short squeeze" at the options level).

Retail Trap Setup (Vol > OI PCR)

  • PCR_vol = 1.60 (retail buying 60% more puts than calls today)
  • PCR_oi = 1.25 (structural: moderate put bias, normal)
  • PCR_div = 0.35 → Gate condition #4 fires
  • Interpretation: retail is panic-buying puts. Market likely reverses upward. Fade the crowd — buy CE if V_CoC is neutral or positive.

Institutional Structural Short (OI > Vol PCR)

  • PCR_oi = 1.70 (structural: heavy put OI, institutions positioned short)
  • PCR_vol = 1.10 (today's retail is balanced, not yet reacting)
  • PCR_div = 0.60 → Gate condition #4 fires
  • Interpretation: institutions built large put positions in prior sessions. Today's move down is structurally supported. Buy PE to follow institutional direction.

Normal PCR Reference Ranges

PCR Value Market Interpretation Notes
< 0.70 Extreme bullishness / call buying frenzy Contrarian bearish signal — market may be topping. Very unusual for NIFTY.
0.70 – 1.00 Mild call bias — slight bullish sentiment Normal range for trending up days.
1.00 – 1.30 Balanced / neutral market No strong directional bias. PCR divergence <0.25 in this zone = no Gate impact.
1.30 – 1.60 Moderate put bias — caution / defensive posture Retail hedging or positioning for downside. Common before known events.
> 1.60 Extreme put buying — fear/panic Contrarian bullish signal unless accompanied by structural OI-PCR at same level.
