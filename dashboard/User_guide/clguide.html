Data Flow — Step by Step

Every five minutes during NSE market hours (first pull 09:16:30 IST, every 5 min until 15:30) the pipeline executes this cycle:

  1. Load watermark
    Reads data/watermark.json — the timestamp of the last successfully pulled record. After the initial backfill, this starts at approximately 2026-02-20 15:30:xx. This is the exclusive lower boundary for every future query.
  2. Pull incremental rows from BigQuery
    Runs: SELECT * FROM upxtx WHERE record_time > '{watermark}' ORDER BY record_time ASC. A typical 5-min snapshot returns ~1,600–2,400 rows covering all index option contracts (CE + PE) and all index and stock futures.
  3. Validate
    Checks schema column presence. Coalesces nulls in ltp using close_price then close. Corrects negative IV values. Logs row counts split by instrument type (OPTIDX, FUTIDX, FUTSTK).
  4. Load or compute ATM window
    Reads today's ATM windows from data/processed/atm_windows/YYYY-MM-DD.json. If this is the first pull of the trading day, computes the window from the opening spot price and saves the JSON for all subsequent pulls to reuse.
  5. Compute all derived columns
    Processor adds: gex_k (per-row GEX contribution), coc (futures carry), moneyness_pct, oi_delta, expiry_tier (TIER1_NEAR / TIER1_FAR / TIER2_IV_ONLY), in_atm_window boolean, obi_raw, snap_time, trade_date.
  6. Write Parquet to disk
    Options: data/raw/options/trade_date=YYYY-MM-DD/UNDERLYING_HHMM.parquet. Futures: data/raw/futures/trade_date=YYYY-MM-DD/ALL_FUTURES_HHMM.parquet. Critical: row_group_size=100,000 with Snappy compression — 3–5× faster DuckDB queries than smaller row groups.
  7. Refresh DuckDB views + advance watermark
    DuckDB glob views (read_parquet('data/raw/options/**/*.parquet')) auto-discover the new file immediately. Watermark advances to the max record_time in this batch and is atomically written to data/watermark.json via a .tmp rename — guaranteeing no partial state on crash.

Concept Library

Every metric on the dashboard is grounded in options market microstructure theory. This section explains each concept from first principles — what it measures, how it is computed, and what the signal means specifically for an option buyer on NSE index derivatives.

Γ

Gamma Exposure (GEX)

MARKET STRUCTURE · DEALER HEDGING MECHANICS · PINNING EFFECT

Every time you buy an option from a market maker, they are now short that option. To avoid taking a directional bet (to remain "delta-neutral"), they must buy or sell the underlying as the spot price moves. Gamma quantifies how much their hedge changes for each one-point move in the underlying. Gamma Exposure (GEX) aggregates this across every single open contract in the market — every strike, every expiry, every underlying — to produce a market-wide portrait of the forces dealers will exert on price.

Think of positive GEX as a gravitational force that pulls spot price back toward the most crowded strike. When GEX is large and positive, every move away from equilibrium forces dealers to trade against it — buying dips and selling rallies — creating a "pinning" or mean-reversion environment. When GEX collapses or turns negative, that gravitational force disappears and directional moves become self-reinforcing rather than self-correcting.

Per-contract GEX contribution (computed in processor.py)
GEX_k = Gamma × OI × Spot² × 0.01 × d_dir

d_dir = +1 for Call options (dealers are short calls → long gamma → counter-trend)
d_dir = −1 for Put options (dealers are short puts → short gamma → trend-following)

Net GEX (computed in analytics.py via DuckDB SQL)
Net_GEX = Σ GEX_k for all Tier 1 strikes and expiries in this snapshot

Expressed in ₹ Billions — represents total dealer gamma book

The dashboard sums all GEX_k values across Tier 1 contracts for each 5-minute snapshot, producing a timeseries of Net GEX throughout the day. The secondary-axis percentage shows how current GEX relates to the day's opening peak — this percentage decay rate is the primary trading signal.

GEX Regimes and Their Practical Effect

Positive GEX — Chop Regime
Dealers long gamma. Every price move triggers dealer trading in the opposite direction. Market mean-reverts. Options buyers' direction is correct but spot doesn't follow through — a frustrating environment.
Negative GEX — Trend Regime
Dealers short gamma. They must buy when price rises and sell when it falls — amplifying every move. Directional options buyers profit fastest in this environment. Large moves are possible and self-sustaining.
GEX Collapse <70% of Peak
Even while still positive, a rapid 30% decline removes the pinning support. This is the transition moment — from chop to trend. Environment Gate awards 2 points for this condition.
💡Best setup pattern: GEX opens high (strong pin early in the session), then drops ≥30% by mid-morning as dealers reduce hedges. This "pin lift" combined with V_CoC < −10 (institutional short campaign) scores 4 of 9 Environment Gate points together and marks the highest-confidence directional setups.

Why GEX and CoC Should Be Read Together

GEX tells you whether the market can move directionally (structural permission). CoC Velocity tells you whether institutions are currently pushing it directionally (actual force). The best trades occur when both align: GEX permission to trend + institutional fuel from V_CoC.

Cost of Carry (CoC) & Velocity (V_CoC)

FUTURES MARKET · INSTITUTIONAL FOOTPRINT DETECTION · LEADING INDICATOR

In a rational market, futures trade at a fair premium above spot equal to the risk-free financing cost for the remaining holding period. This is the Cost of Carry. For NIFTY near-month futures, fair carry is typically +30 to +80 index points depending on days-to-expiry and the prevailing repo rate. When the actual futures price deviates significantly from fair carry, it signals that large participants are aggressively establishing directional positions.

Institutions (FIIs, prop desks, large mutual funds) prefer to build directional positions in futures first for two reasons: (1) futures have lower impact cost per rupee of notional compared to options, and (2) futures carry no IV risk, making the entry cost predictable. By monitoring how quickly CoC changes, we effectively observe institutional order flow in real-time — before it shows up in price.

Why V_CoC Leads Spot by 60–90 Minutes

The institutional order flow sequence is: Futures (lowest impact cost) → Delta hedging in options (as their option positions build) → Actual spot price movement (as retail follows). By the time spot visibly falls, V_CoC already warned 60–90 minutes earlier. This lead time is the core edge that CoC Velocity provides to option buyers.

Implied Volatility (IV)

OPTIONS PRICING · THE PRICE OF UNCERTAINTY · BUYER'S COST BASIS

An option's price is determined by several factors: the underlying's current price, the strike price, time to expiry, the risk-free rate, and — crucially — the market's expectation of future volatility. Implied Volatility is this volatility expectation, reverse-engineered from the observed option price using the Black-Scholes model. It represents the annualised standard deviation of returns that the option price implies the market expects the underlying to exhibit before expiry.

If NIFTY is at 25,000 and ATM IV is 14%, the option market is saying: "We expect NIFTY to experience approximately ±14% annualised volatility." For practical trading, this translates to:

Expected move over N trading days
Expected Move = Spot × IV% × √(N / 252)

For 1 day: 25,000 × 0.14 × √(1/252) ≈ ±220 points
For 7 days: 25,000 × 0.14 × √(7/252) ≈ ±584 points

Option buyer's break-even movement
Break-even Move = Option Premium / Delta

If you pay ₹20 for a delta-0.35 option, spot must move ≈₹57 in your favour to break even at expiry

As an option buyer, IV is your primary cost driver. You do not just buy direction — you buy direction at a price set by the IV market. When IV is high (IVP >75%), you are paying more than usual for the same probability of profit, meaning the underlying needs to move more than the market already expects just to break even. When IV is low (IVP <30%), you are buying inexpensive insurance — even a moderate directional move generates attractive returns.

IV Skew — Why Puts Are Almost Always More Expensive

In index options, put IVs are consistently higher than call IVs at the same distance from spot. This "negative skew" or "volatility smile" exists because institutional players permanently demand downside hedging (puts) as portfolio insurance, creating structural excess demand that inflates put premiums relative to their theoretical Black-Scholes value. The IV Surface panel makes this visible: PE strikes are always brighter (higher IV) than equidistant CE strikes.

This skew has a practical implication: when buying puts for a bearish view, you are paying a structural premium. When buying calls for a bullish view, you are getting a relative discount. Factor this into your strike selection.

Historically cheap options. Every point of directional movement earns more than it would at high IV. Maximum structural edge for buyers.

IV Rank (IVR) & IV Percentile (IVP)

VOLATILITY CONTEXT · HISTORICAL COMPARISON · TIMING INDICATOR

Knowing that today's NIFTY ATM IV is 14% tells you nothing actionable without historical context. Is 14% cheap, fair, or expensive for NIFTY? IVR and IVP both answer this by anchoring today's IV within its recent historical range — but they do so in fundamentally different ways, and understanding that difference is important.

IV Rank (IVR) — position within the 90-day range
IVR = (IV_today − IV_90d_low) / (IV_90d_high − IV_90d_low) × 100

0% = IV at its 90-day low (cheapest possible)
100% = IV at its 90-day high (most expensive possible)
50% = halfway between recent extremes

IV Percentile (IVP) — fraction of days below today's IV
IVP = Count(past 90 trading days where closing IV < today's IV) / 90 × 100

IVP = 25% means IV was lower than today on only 25% of the past 90 days
→ today is in the 75th percentile = relatively expensive

IVP = 25% means IV was lower on 25 of 90 days
→ today is in the lower 25% = relatively cheap
IVR — Simple but Outlier-Sensitive

IVR uses only the min and max of the range. One extreme event (a market crash, a budget day spike) can distort the entire calculation. Example: IV spiked to 42% during a flash crash 80 days ago. Today it's at 16%. IVR calculates: (16−10)/(42−10) = 18.75% — suggesting cheap, even though 16% is actually elevated for normal conditions. IVR is useful as a quick sanity check but not reliable for precise entry timing.

IVP — Robust, Preferred by Dashboard

IVP is more stable, more robust to outliers, and more reliable for the kind of systematic threshold (IVP <50%) used in the Environment Gate. This is why the dashboard uses IVP for Condition 5 scoring, not IVR.

Put-Call Ratio (PCR) & Divergence

SENTIMENT ANALYSIS · RETAIL vs INSTITUTIONAL FLOW · CONTRARIAN SIGNAL

The Put-Call Ratio measures the relative activity in put versus call options. A PCR >1 conventionally signals bearish sentiment. The dashboard goes significantly deeper by tracking two distinct PCRs simultaneously — one reflecting current intraday retail activity and one reflecting accumulated institutional positioning — and mining the gap between them for contrarian signals.

PCR Volume (fast — intraday retail sentiment)
PCR_Vol = Σ PE_volume / Σ CE_volume    # computed on each 5-min snapshot individually

PCR OI (slow — accumulated institutional positioning)
PCR_OI = Σ PE_open_interest / Σ CE_open_interest    # accumulated over days

PCR Divergence (the actionable signal)
Divergence = PCR_Vol − PCR_OI

Positive divergence: retail buying more puts than their OI position suggests
Negative divergence: retail buying more calls than their OI position suggests

The Mechanics of the Retail Trap

Open Interest accumulates slowly and patiently as institutions build multi-day positions. A large institutional put position in OI represents a conviction trade that took hours or days to establish. When retail traders — reacting to a news headline, a broker alert, or social media panic — suddenly rush to buy puts intraday, PCR_Vol spikes while PCR_OI remains stable.

This divergence historically marks a local price low, not a continuing sell-off. Institutions, who are already positioned with puts, are not adding at this moment of retail panic — they are often covering, taking profits, or even reversing. The retail crowd is buying puts at exactly the wrong moment. This is the "retail trap" that the divergence signal detects.

Divergence >+0.30 (Gate: +1 pt)
Retail panic-buying puts. Institutions calm or covering. Classic retail trap → local bottom forming. Spot typically bounces within 2–6 candles. Contrarian CE signal.
Divergence <−0.20
Retail rushing into calls against institutional put positioning. Spot tends to fall as institutional hedges exercise. Contrarian PE signal.
Divergence between −0.20 and +0.25
Retail and institutional flows broadly consistent. No contrarian trap forming. Direction from GEX and CoC instead.

Option Greeks

PRICE SENSITIVITY MEASURES · RISK QUANTIFICATION

The Greeks quantify how sensitive an option's price is to changes in each underlying factor — spot price, volatility, time, and interest rates. They are pre-computed by the BigQuery data source and used throughout the dashboard for GEX calculation, S_score ranking, P&L decomposition, and stop-loss computation.

Taylor Series P&L Decomposition (Position Monitor)

The Position Monitor uses a second-order Taylor expansion to break down every 5-minute P&L change into its Greek components:

ΔV ≈ Δ·ΔS + ½Γ·(ΔS)² + ν·Δσ + Θ·Δt

ΔS = spot price change in this 5-min bar
Δσ = implied volatility change in this 5-min bar
Δt = 5/375 (each bar = 1/75 of the 375-minute trading day)
Unexplained = Actual ΔV − Theoretical ΔV (higher-order residual)

This breakdown tells you exactly why your P&L changed. If Delta P&L is positive but Vega P&L is deeply negative, IV compressed against you as spot moved in your favour — a common frustration on high-IVP entry days. If Theta P&L dominates the losses, the position is structurally decaying faster than spot is moving — time to exit.

Slippage-Adjusted S_score

STRIKE SELECTION · EFFICIENCY RANKING · ORDER BOOK QUALITY

The S_score answers: "Among all available strikes within my universe, which one gives the best ratio of directional exposure per unit of daily decay cost, after accounting for the real-world cost of actually executing the trade?" It is the sole ranking criterion in the Strike Screener.

A naive efficiency metric like Delta/Theta ignores a critical practical reality: option markets have varying liquidity. A strike that looks highly efficient on paper might have a bid-ask spread of 5–10% of premium, completely destroying the theoretical advantage. S_score incorporates an order book quality penalty that collapses the score of illiquid strikes toward zero — steering you away from attractive-looking but unexecutable trades.

Step 1 — Raw Efficiency Ratio
EffRatio = |Delta| / |Theta|

Interpretation: directional exposure (₹ per 1-pt move) per ₹ of daily decay cost
Higher is better. ATM options typically 0.05–0.12 for near-expiry.

Step 2 — Slippage Penalty ρ (order book quality)
ρ = exp(−2 × TBQ × TSQ / ((TBQ + TSQ) × V_bench))

TBQ = total_buy_qty from BigQuery schema (order book bid side depth)
TSQ = total_sell_qty from BigQuery schema (order book ask side depth)
V_bench = 2,000,000 (benchmark order book size for liquid index options)

ρ → 0 when book is deep and balanced → negligible slippage penalty
ρ → 1 when book is thin or one-sided → score collapses toward zero

Step 3 — Final S_score
S_score = EffRatio × (1 − ρ)

If ρ = 0 (perfect liquidity): S_score = EffRatio
If ρ = 0.5 (moderate slippage): S_score = 0.5 × EffRatio
If ρ = 0.9 (severe slippage): S_score = 0.1 × EffRatio (nearly unusable)

How the Slippage Penalty Works Mechanically

The formula uses the harmonic mean principle of TBQ and TSQ relative to V_bench. When both bid and ask sides are deep (TBQ ≈ TSQ ≈ 2,000,000), the product TBQ × TSQ / (TBQ + TSQ) is large relative to V_bench, the exponent becomes a large negative number, and ρ approaches zero. When one side is thin (e.g. TSQ = 50,000) or both are thin (TBQ = TSQ = 100,000), the fraction is small, the exponent is near zero, and ρ approaches one — warning you away from the illiquid strike.

Order Book Imbalance (OBI)

MICROSTRUCTURE · REAL-TIME PRESSURE MEASUREMENT · LEADING INDICATOR

Order Book Imbalance measures the real-time balance of buying versus selling pressure in the order book at any moment. Unlike volume (which records trades that already happened), OBI reflects what is queued to happen next — making it a genuine leading indicator of short-term price pressure.

The BigQuery schema provides total_buy_qty and total_sell_qty per row — these are the aggregated bid and ask side depths in the order book at the time of the snapshot. Their imbalance tells you whether buyers or sellers are more aggressive in queuing orders.

Raw OBI per contract row
OBI = (total_buy_qty − total_sell_qty) / (total_buy_qty + total_sell_qty)

Range: −1.0 to +1.0
+1.0 = all bids, zero asks (extreme buy pressure — buyers desperate to fill)
−1.0 = all asks, zero bids (extreme sell pressure — sellers dumping)
0.0 = perfectly balanced

Smoothed OBI (5-snapshot, 25-minute rolling average)
Smoothed_OBI(t) = Mean(OBI(t), OBI(t−1), OBI(t−2), OBI(t−3), OBI(t−4))

The raw per-snapshot OBI is noisy. A 5-snapshot (25-minute) rolling average smooths out momentary spikes and reveals sustained directional pressure. This smoothed value is what feeds Environment Gate Conditions 3 (futures OBI) and 6 (options OBI).

Smoothed Options OBI <−0.15 (Gate: +1 pt)
Sustained sell-side aggression in the options order book. Sellers consistently hitting bids. Directional sellers dominating options flow. Bearish.
Smoothed Futures OBI <−0.10 (Gate: +1 pt)
Supply pressure in futures order book. Large participants placing sell orders aggressively. Often precedes V_CoC turning negative.
Smoothed OBI >+0.15
Buy-side pressure dominant. Buyers aggressively lifting offers. Consistent with upward momentum. Bullish structural bias.
|Smoothed OBI| <0.10
Balanced order flow. No dominant pressure from either side. Market in equilibrium — other signals needed for direction.

OBI vs Volume — Why Both Matter

Volume tells you about completed trades (historical). OBI tells you about unfilled orders (current intention). A large volume surge with OBI near zero means both buyers and sellers traded actively — net pressure was balanced. A large volume surge with deeply negative OBI means sellers were dominant — a directionally meaningful reading. The combination of high volume + negative OBI is a strong bearish signal.

Theta-Adjusted Trailing Stop Loss

RISK MANAGEMENT · POSITION MONITORING · GREEK-AWARE EXIT

A conventional percentage stop loss (e.g. "exit if premium falls 30% from entry") ignores a fundamental property of options: premium decay is inevitable, even if the underlying doesn't move at all. Theta is a known, predictable component of premium loss — it is not the risk you are trying to stop-out. A tight fixed stop will repeatedly exit valid positions due to normal theta decay alone, not because the directional thesis was wrong.

The Theta-Adjusted Trailing Stop Loss solves this by lowering the stop floor proportionally to expected daily theta decay. At any point in the trading day, your SL reflects only the risk you care about — adverse spot movement — not the inescapable passage of time.

Step 1 — Base SL (computed once at trade entry)
SL_base = Entry_LTP × (1 − Max_Loss_Fraction)

Example: Entry at ₹19.95, Max_Loss = 30%
SL_base = 19.95 × 0.70 = ₹13.97

Step 2 — Theta Adjustment (updated every 5 min)
SL_adj(t) = SL_base + Theta_daily × (t_elapsed_minutes / 375)

375 = total NSE trading minutes (09:15:00 to 15:30:00)
Theta_daily is negative → SL_adj decreases over time (moves lower)

Step 3 — Exit trigger
If LTP < SL_adj(t) → STOP_HIT → exit immediately

Worked Example

You buy a NIFTY PE at 09:20 for ₹19.95. Theta = −6.63/day. Max_Loss = 30%.

The exit at 14:30 is triggered by the time rule, not the SL.

ATM Window

STRIKE UNIVERSE · DAY-OPEN ANCHOR · DAILY CONSISTENCY

The ATM Window defines the exact set of strikes included in all dashboard analytics for each underlying every day. It is anchored to the day-opening spot price from the 09:15 snapshot — not the current live price. This ensures the strike universe is completely consistent from 09:15 to 15:30, making cross-snapshot comparisons meaningful.

Step 1 — Day-open spot from first snapshot
Day_Open_Spot = underlying_spot from earliest record_time on trade_date

Step 2 — Round to nearest strike interval
ATM_Strike = ROUND(Day_Open_Spot / Strike_Interval) × Strike_Interval

Step 3 — Window bounds (±8 strikes)
Lower = ATM_Strike − (8 × Strike_Interval)
Upper = ATM_Strike + (8 × Strike_Interval)

Watermark — Incremental Pull State

STATE MANAGEMENT · IDEMPOTENCY · CRASH RESILIENCE

The watermark is a single timestamp in data/watermark.json recording the last moment successfully pulled from BigQuery. Every incremental pull queries for records newer than this timestamp, then advances it to the latest record time in the new batch.

This provides three critical properties: (1) No duplicates — the same records are never pulled twice. (2) No gaps — every record between old and new watermark is always captured. (3) Crash resilience — if the pipeline crashes mid-pull before writing Parquet, the watermark has not advanced yet, so the next run re-pulls that batch safely.

Watermark file format
{"watermark": "2026-02-23 09:21:00"}   # IST, no timezone suffix, no Z, no +05:30

BigQuery pull query using watermark
SELECT * FROM `universal-ion-437606-b7.bgquery.upxtx`
WHERE record_time > TIMESTAMP("2026-02-23 09:21:00")
ORDER BY record_time ASC

Watermark saved atomically (macOS APFS)
Write to data/watermark.json.tmp → Path.replace() → data/watermark.json
⚠️Never manually edit watermark.json unless you know exactly what you are doing. Setting it backward causes duplicate data; setting it forward creates a gap in historical data. To diagnose a suspected gap, compare Parquet filenames (HHMM) against the watermark timestamp.

Trading Workflow — Six Phases

The dashboard is built around a structured daily routine. Consistency in following this sequence matters more than any individual signal. The system's edge comes from discipline applied over many trades, not from any single perfect call.

ℹ️Verify your Mac clock shows IST before each trading day: run date in Terminal. The pipeline's market-hours detection uses local system time — if it is off by even one minute, the first pull may be missed.
PHASE 1

Pre-Market Setup (09:00–09:15 IST)

The 15 minutes before market open establish your context for the day. Do not enter the session without reviewing yesterday's conditions and setting your bias.

  • Open Terminal tabs 1, 2, 3. Start pipeline → API → dashboard in that order. Wait 60 seconds between each for initialisation.
  • Verify at :8000/docs (15 endpoints visible) and :5173 (panels load with no error banners).
  • Select yesterday's date. Review closing GEX trend: rising into close (pinned = chop expected tomorrow morning) or collapsing (directional continuation possible)?
  • Note yesterday's closing CoC: in premium (neutral) or in discount (sellers dominated into close)? Discount CoC into yesterday's close = bearish carry-over today.
  • Check IVP level: <30% → structural buying advantage today. >70% → plan debit spreads, not naked longs.
  • Check pre-market macro: SGX NIFTY direction, US overnight close, RBI or major news. Large gap expectations shift ATM window significantly at open.
  • Set a directional prior: CE-biased, PE-biased, or neutral. Write it down. The dashboard will confirm or contradict it — both are useful information.

ATM Window

STRIKE UNIVERSE · DAY-OPEN ANCHOR · DAILY CONSISTENCY

The ATM Window defines the exact set of strikes included in all dashboard analytics for each underlying every day. It is anchored to the day-opening spot price from the 09:15 snapshot — not the current live price. This ensures the strike universe is completely consistent from 09:15 to 15:30, making cross-snapshot comparisons meaningful.

Step 1 — Day-open spot from first snapshot
Day_Open_Spot = underlying_spot from earliest record_time on trade_date

Step 2 — Round to nearest strike interval
ATM_Strike = ROUND(Day_Open_Spot / Strike_Interval) × Strike_Interval

Step 3 — Window bounds (±8 strikes)
Lower = ATM_Strike − (8 × Strike_Interval)
Upper = ATM_Strike + (8 × Strike_Interval)

BANKNIFTY (interval=100): 17 strikes, 1,600-point range
FINNIFTY (interval=50): 17 strikes, 800-point range
MIDCPNIFTY (interval=25): 17 strikes, 400-point range
NIFTYNXT50 (interval=50): 17 strikes, 800-point range

The window file is computed from the first pull of each day and saved immediately to data/processed/atm_windows/YYYY-MM-DD.json. It is never updated mid-day — the same fixed window applies from 09:15:00 to 15:30:00.

Watermark — Incremental Pull State

STATE MANAGEMENT · IDEMPOTENCY · CRASH RESILIENCE

The watermark is a single timestamp in data/watermark.json recording the last moment successfully pulled from BigQuery. Every incremental pull queries for records newer than this timestamp, then advances it to the latest record time in the new batch.

This provides three critical properties: (1) No duplicates — the same records are never pulled twice. (2) No gaps — every record between old and new watermark is always captured. (3) Crash resilience — if the pipeline crashes mid-pull before writing Parquet, the watermark has not advanced yet, so the next run re-pulls that batch safely.

Watermark file format
{"watermark": "2026-02-23 09:21:00"}   # IST, no timezone suffix, no Z, no +05:30

BigQuery pull query using watermark
SELECT * FROM `universal-ion-437606-b7.bgquery.upxtx`
WHERE record_time > TIMESTAMP("2026-02-23 09:21:00")
ORDER BY record_time ASC

Watermark saved atomically (macOS APFS)
Write to data/watermark.json.tmp → Path.replace() → data/watermark.json
⚠️Never manually edit watermark.json unless you know exactly what you are doing. Setting it backward causes duplicate data; setting it forward creates a gap in historical data. To diagnose a suspected gap, compare Parquet filenames (HHMM) against the watermark timestamp.

  • If PE is profitable (+30%+) and a strong contra-signal appears: consider booking 50% to lock gains before a potential bounce against you.

PHASE 5

Exit Rules — Strict Priority Order

Exit rules are enforced in the order listed. Rule 1 always overrides Rules 2–5. Never override Rule 1 for any reason — it is a hard system rule, not a discretionary guideline.

RULE 1
Stop Loss Hit (absolute, non-negotiable): Position Monitor shows STOP_HIT in red. LTP dropped below SL_adj. Exit the entire remaining position at market immediately. No waiting for a bounce. No averaging down. No exceptions. The system has confirmed the directional thesis is wrong. This is the most important discipline in the entire workflow.
RULE 2
Environment Gate Collapse: Gate drops from GO to NO-GO (0–1 pts) while in a position. All structural conditions that justified your entry have reversed. Exit 75–100% of position immediately. The edge is gone entirely.
RULE 3
Gate Weakening: Gate drops from GO to WAIT (2–3 pts). Exit 50% of position. Raise SL on remaining 50% to entry LTP (free trade). The edge has diminished but has not reversed completely.
RULE 4
100% Profit Target: LTP reaches 2× entry. Book 50% of lots at market. Raise SL on remaining lots to entry LTP. This locks in your profit and creates a risk-free remainder.
RULE 5
Time Exit at 14:30 IST (hard deadline): Close all remaining positions by 14:30 regardless of P&L. Theta decay accelerates sharply in the final 60 minutes for near-expiry options. The risk-reward of holding deteriorates rapidly. Even profitable positions should be exited by 14:30 on TIER1_NEAR contracts. This is not optional.
ℹ️Why 14:30 and not 15:25? For a NIFTY weekly option with 3 DTE, the theta per minute in the final hour is approximately 3–4× the theta per minute earlier in the day. A position worth ₹20 at 14:30 may be worth only ₹8–12 at 15:25 with zero spot movement. The liquidity also deteriorates as dealers reduce risk into close. Exit while the market is still orderly.
PHASE 6

End-of-Day Review (15:30+ IST)

After market close and all positions are closed, spend 10–15 minutes reviewing today's session. Improvement as a systematic trader comes from post-session analysis, not from trading more.

  • GEX Chart replay: At what time did GEX peak? When did it collapse below 70%? Did a large spot move correlate with that timing? Was the signal early enough to have traded?
  • Alert Feed review: All today's alerts are preserved. Were COC_VELOCITY alerts accurate? Were RETAIL_PANIC alerts followed by a bounce? Build your own statistics over weeks.
  • P&L attribution in Position Monitor: What percentage came from Delta vs Vega vs Theta? A vega-heavy gain means IV expansion was the main driver — less predictable. A delta-heavy gain means the spot move was the driver — more aligned with the thesis.
  • Gate accuracy check: Did you enter at Gate ≥4? Did the trade move in the predicted direction within the first 30 minutes? Over time, track your win rate vs Gate score to calibrate the system to your own trading style.
  • No-trade day validation: If Gate never reached 4 and you correctly did not trade, record this as a green day — capital preserved is as valuable as capital gained.
  • Close Terminal tabs. Data is safe on disk. No need to stop any database or clean up files. Everything persists safely.

Maximum Risk per Trade

Never risk more than 2% of total trading capital on any single option position. With the theta-adjusted SL set at a maximum 30% loss from entry, calculate your maximum lot size as:

Max_Risk_₹ = Total_Capital × 0.02
SL_Loss_₹ = Entry_LTP × 0.30 × Lot_Size
Max_Lots = floor(Max_Risk_₹ / SL_Loss_₹)

Example: Capital = ₹5,00,000. Entry LTP = ₹20. NIFTY lot size = 75 units.
Max_Risk = ₹10,000
SL_Loss per lot = ₹20 × 0.30 × 75 = ₹450
Max_Lots = floor(10,000 / 450) = 22 lots at full Gate score
At Gate score 4 (50% fraction): 11 lots
